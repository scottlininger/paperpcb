Getting started

PCBs at home?

Yup, it's possible, at least for simple projects. If you're an electronics newb, Arduino nut, or makerspace mama, these techniques might be for you.

If your PCB project has:

  • one or two layers
  • thickish traces (8mil+, .2mm+)
  • not too many holes and vias

and you're willing to buy some copper tape and a $75-$200 Cricut or Silhouette machine, read on!

Visual learner? Watch the getting started video. 

(image of the layers)

Make printed circuit boards in a few easy-ish steps:

1. Design it#

Using your favorite design tool (KiCad, EasyEDA, Photoshop, InkSpace, GIMP, etc.) draw your circuit board. Save your layers as Gerber, PSD, PNG, or SVG. 

(kicad I guess? export arrow to gerber etc. ms paint joke?)

Your design can be "just traces", single or double sided, solder masked, flexible, transparent, and more. See the Designing section for all the options.

2. Convert it#

Drag your files into the free PaperPCB Studio and tell it what kind of board you want.

(image of the design tool, drag in, export out)

The software aligns everything nicely, then exports a printable PDF and cuttable SVG.

3. Print it#

This step is optional, but if you have a laser or inkjet printer, this is how you get graphics on your board. Print the PDF you downloaded a second ago.

(printer output example)

In addition to your jazzy art, the page includes registration marks so the cutting will be pixel-perfect.

If you don't have a printer, no worries. You can use blank paper, cardstock, kapton tape, etc.

4. Copper it#

This is the step that adds the spark. Peel and stick some copper tape to your page. (See the what you need page for tips on buying the stuff.) 

(photo or video of applying copper)

If you did printing step #3, the copper areas will be nicely marked. If you didn't, you'll have to eyeball it. It's not too hard.

5. Cut it#

At this point, you'll have some coppered paper and the SVG file you got from Studio. Import it into your cutter's control software. 

(cutting cricut video)

Now load your mat and get cutting! See the Machines section for the best settings to use.

6. Weed it#

Cutting's done! Carefully peel away the unwanted copper to leave your traces. 

(weeding img or video)

This step requires a steady hand and a little patience, but if you choose the right paper, the copper will peel right off. See the Materials section for links to recommended papers and shopping tips.

7. Stab it#

If your design has holes or vias, poke them through the paper with a needle or punch. (Of course, if your design is all surface-mount, you can skip this step.) 

(stabbing img or video)

Since most pin-based modules are designed for breadboards, the holes have standard tenth-inch spacing. Save time by 3d printing the official PaperPCB tool, Mr. Stabby. He's fun and only a little dangerous.

That's it!#

Or at least... that's the basic idea. Now go solder up your board and test it! If you're like me, your design will need a lot of iteration. I hope PaperPCB helps with that.
